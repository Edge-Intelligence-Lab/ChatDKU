Professor awarded 1921 Prize in American Literature

Dr. Selina Lai-Henderson, associate professor of American literature and history, won the 1921 Prize in American Literature in the "tenured" category with her article, "You Are No Darker Than I Am: The Souls of Black Folk in Maoist China". The award-winning essay examines W. E. B. Du Bois's The Souls of Black Folk (1903) outside the historical and racial context of the United States by exploring the impact of the book's Chinese translation in 1959 China in light of Du Bois's historic visit to the country in the same year. Situated in the critical formation of Afro-Asian engagements during the Bandung era, Du Bois's work was pivotal to China's reassertion of what it means to be "black" on the global stage of proletariat revolution. Awarded annually for "the best article in any field of American literary studies" by the American Literature Society (ALS), the prize is named for the year the ALS was founded to "promote and diversify the study of American Literature."

Faculty launch new book on independent language learning 

Three faculty launched their book – "How Do You Learn a Language? Action Plans for Independent Language Learning" – in the Performance Cafe on campus.
DKU's Dr. Donald Snow and Zhang Minghao (Rainie) together with Shuhan Li from Xi'an Jiaotong Liverpool University shared how the project came about and their personal experiences of learning a second language.

The book combines their own encounters as learners with theoretical research. The authors told the event that independent language learning does not have to be a purely solo endeavor, as they encouraged people to seek out support from others.

During a Q&A session at the Jan. 12 launch, the authors emphasized the importance of motivation in learning a second language, given that most people do so without being immersed in the environment of the target language. They said they hoped the book would provide practical advice and feasible steps in a simple and clear manner for those actively self-learning a second language.

Examining the future of global health research and education

The most urgent global health questions to address by 2030 and new DKU research into population aging were discussed at a collaborative workshop organized by the Duke Kunshan Global Health Research Center and the School of Public Health at Wuhan University (WHU).

Guest speakers and representatives from about 10 universities – including DKU, WHU, the University of Richmond, Zhejiang University, Sun Yat-sen University, Xi'an Jiaotong University, Lanzhou University and Jiujiang University – also looked at the future of global health education and presented their latest research findings. The workshop was co-hosted in December by Dr. Yunguo Liu, co-director of the GHRC, and Dr. Hao Li, director of the international program at WHU's School of Public Health.
