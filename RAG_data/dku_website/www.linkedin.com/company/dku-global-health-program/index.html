About us

Duke Kunshan University and the Duke Global Health Institute established the Global Health Research Center in 2013. The Center uses innovative approaches to research and solve important health and health policy challenges in China and around the world. The Global Health Research Center is built on the principles of scientific excellence and focuses on establishing interdisciplinary, mutually-beneficial collaborations with researchers from China. It will host national and international conferences to disseminate research findings and encourage dialogue among scientists, professionals, policy-makers and other key stakeholders engaged in global health. The faculty and research activities complement the global health education programs offered at DKU, including the Master of Science in Global Health and the undergraduate semester courses in global health.

    Humans can get their cats sick with seasonal flu A recent study by Dr. Sajid Umar and his One Health team at DKU Global Health Research Center reveals new evidence of reverse zoonosis. The study found that close contact between cat owners and their cats can lead to the spread of influenza. Influenza A viruses (IVA) infecting cats might be just the beginning of pathogens transmitted from humans to animals. The research team collected 458 nasal swabs from sick cats in Kunshan, Jiangsu, finding IVA strains in 2.8% of samples. Most cats live in close proximity to humans, which unfortunately creates more opportunities for pathogen transmission between humans and cats, potentially leading to the emergence of new pathogenic strains or variants. This highlights the urgent need for comprehensive research and preventive measures in reverse zoonotic diseases. Key recommendations include vaccinating both cat owners and their pets, minimizing contact during flu infections, and maintaining hygiene. Enhanced testing and surveillance at the human-animal contact interface are essential to prevent future influenza pandemics. Stay informed and protect your pets this flu season!     April 24th kicks off the World Immunization Week this year. China is one of the first countries in the world to launch an immunization program with the most extensive coverage. Its National Immunization Program (NIP) provides 14 vaccines against 15 diseases, including hepatitis B and polio, covering more than 95% of children under six nationwide. However, challenges remain especially on the low coverage and inequitable access of several WHO highly recommended life-saving vaccines. In recognizing China’s efforts to save countless lives from vaccine-preventable diseases and calling on further investments in the NIP to protect the next generations, the DKU Innovation Lab for Vaccine Delivery Research published a thematic series - Advancing the National Immunization Program in China: Making it More Effective and Sustainable – in the BMC Infectious Disease of Poverty. The collection is an output of VaxLab’s ongoing work, providing an overview of immunization in China while setting the stage for future research and policy advocacy to achieve universal vaccine coverage.     Recently, Professor Sarah Cook, Head of the School of Economics at FHSS, was invited to the No.193 Aegon-Industrial Global Health Symposium and delivered a compelling speech titled “Gender, health, and development: Insights for a feminist economy of well-being” at Duke Kunshan University. This presentation delved into the concept of a feminist economy of well-being and explored how progress towards such an economy could be measured. In an era where there's a growing call to look 'Beyond GDP', it's crucial to understand the limitations of GDP growth as a measure of economic well-being. Health plays a pivotal role in this discourse, serving as both an input and an outcome of development. Integrating a feminist perspective into these discussions involves examining how gender structures the economy, including the gender division of labour, responsibilities for social reproduction, and the impact of different roles on women’s health and well-being. The talk sparked insightful discussions among attendees, shedding light on the importance of redefining measures of progress beyond traditional GDP metrics. #FHSS #GenderEquality #EconomicWellBeing #FeministEconomy

    Today is the Chinese National Love Your Nose Day—a national day for promoting awareness of nasal diseases. A recent policy brief, co-authored by researchers from Global Health Research Center and clinicians from Shanghai Jiao Tong University, underscores the urgent need for comprehensive strategies to manage allergic rhinitis, one of the most common chronic respiratory diseases in China, particularly affecting adolescents and children. Despite allergic rhinitis’s high burden, there is inadequate focus on its research and prevention, a shortage of specialists, insufficient knowledge among general practitioners, and a lack of public awareness about the condition. The brief calls for heightened awareness and the establishment of a robust, multi-faceted prevention system that incorporates the public health, environmental protection, meteorology, finance, and medical insurance sectors. The proposed policy recommendations include enhancing allergy specialists training, raising disease awareness among the general public, and expanding the cross-sector collaboration in more provinces. Join us in advocating for these vital changes to better support those affected by allergic rhinitis, reducing the hidden health burden and creating a healthier future for all. #AllergicRhinitis #DKUGlobalHealth #DKU #DukeKunshanUniversity #DukeUniversity #Duke #GlobalHealth #全球健康 #昆山杜克大学 #昆杜 #昆山
